The diary was the only place she was fully honest — because the Philippines doesn't always make it easy to say out loud that you're not okay. Her debut wishes and her depression exist on the same pages, in the same handwriting, because that's the truth of it: you can still want things while you're drowning. You can still plan a party while you're disappearing.
The mother reads all of it. She doesn't skip the hard parts. She honors the whole daughter — not just the one who wanted red roses, but the one who was afraid she wouldn't make it to eighteen and wrote it down because there was nowhere else to put it.

The Diary — Both Layers
Each entry has two parts written on the same page in the same hand. The debut wish, and underneath it — sometimes the same night, sometimes days later — the truth she couldn't say to anyone's face.
The mother reads all of it aloud. The player hears everything.

Entry 1 — The Roses
The wish:
"Gusto ko ng red roses. Eighteen. Alam ko masasabi ni Mama na mahal, pero debut ko lang naman ito. Red para dramatic. Red para parang importante ka — kahit sandali lang."
(I want red roses. Eighteen. I know Mama will say they're expensive, but it's only my debut. Red to feel dramatic. Red to feel important — even just for a moment.)
The truth, written three days later in smaller letters:
"Minsan parang wala akong kulay. Hindi ko alam kung paano ipaliwanag yun. Lahat ng tao okay lang, ako lang parang nanonood sa labas ng bintana. Kaya siguro gusto ko ng red. Para maramdaman ko na may kulay pa rin ang mundo. Na may kulay pa rin ako."
(Sometimes I feel like I have no color. I don't know how to explain that. Everyone else is fine, and I'm just watching from outside a window. Maybe that's why I want red. To feel like the world still has color. Like I still have color.)

Entry 2 — The Dress
The wish:
"Simple lang ang gusto ko. White. Basta parang AKO ang nakasuot — hindi yung dress ang nagsusuot sa akin. Gusto ko para isang beses, totoo ang makita ng lahat."
(I just want something simple. White. As long as it feels like I'M wearing it — not the dress wearing me. I want people to see something real, for once.)
The truth:
"Hindi ko alam kung sino ako pag wala ang lahat ng pinagsasabi ng iba. Palagi akong kumikilos para sa ibang tao — ngumingiti para hindi sila mag-alala, nagsasabi ng 'okay lang' para hindi sila maabala. Pero pag mag-isa ako, hindi ko alam kung anong mukha ang totoong mukha ko. Gusto ko ng simpleng dress dahil pagod na ako magpanggap na mas malaki ako sa tunay kong nararamdaman."
(I don't know who I am without everything other people say I should be. I'm always performing for others — smiling so they won't worry, saying 'I'm fine' so they won't be bothered. But when I'm alone, I don't know which face is my real face. I want a simple dress because I'm tired of pretending to be bigger than what I actually feel.)

Entry 3 — The Candles
The wish:
"Eighteen candles. Isa-isa silang sinisindi — hindi lahat sabay. Para may sandali bawat taon. Para may liwanag na dumadating nang dahan-dahan."
(Eighteen candles. Lit one by one — not all at once. So each year gets a moment. So the light comes slowly.)
The truth, written at the bottom of the same page in different ink — like she came back to it:
"Minsan hindi ko alam kung aabot ako ng 18. Hindi ko ibig sabihin yun ng masama — basta ganun lang ang nararamdaman ko. Parang malayo ang 18. Parang imposible. Nagsusulat pa rin ako ng gusto ko dahil gusto kong maniwala na kaya ko. Na aabot ako. Pero takot ako. Takot na takot ako na hindi ako maabot ng liwanag."
(Sometimes I don't know if I'll make it to 18. I don't mean it in a bad way — it just feels that way. Like 18 is far. Like it's impossible. I'm still writing what I want because I want to believe I can do it. That I'll get there. But I'm scared. I'm so scared that the light won't reach me.)

When the mother reads this entry, she stops after the last line. She doesn't speak for a moment. Then she lights the first candle. Then the second. She lights all eighteen — slowly, one by one — and does not stop until the room is full of light.

Entry 4 — The Song
The wish:
"Yung kanta namin ni Mama sa kusina. Hindi kami pareho ng tono pero sabay-sabay pa rin kami. Yun lang ang gusto ko. Wala nang iba."
(Me and Mama's kitchen song. We're both off-key but we sing it together anyway. That's all I want. Nothing else.)
The truth:
"Hindi ko masabi kay Mama. Ilang beses na akong nagtatangkang sabihin — 'Ma, hindi ako okay' — pero pag nandoon na siya, nababago ang isip ko. Ayaw ko siyang pabigatin. Ayaw ko siyang inisin. Ayaw ko siyang makita na nag-aalala dahil sa akin. Kaya ang ginagawa ko, kinakanta ko lang yung kanta namin sa isip ko pag masama ang pakiramdam ko. Para parang nandoon siya. Para parang hindi ako nag-iisa. Pero nag-iisa ako, Mama. Nag-iisa talaga ako at hindi ko alam kung paano sabihin yun."
(I can't tell Mama. I've tried so many times — 'Ma, I'm not okay' — but when she's there, I change my mind. I don't want to burden her. I don't want to bother her. I don't want to see her worry because of me. So what I do is I sing our kitchen song in my head when I'm feeling bad. So it feels like she's there. So it feels like I'm not alone. But I'm alone, Mama. I'm really alone and I don't know how to say that.)

The mother's voice does not break when she reads this. She has already cried over this entry — many times, alone, before today. She has practiced being steady for this. She hums the song after she finishes reading. She hums the whole thing through.

Entry 5 — The Crown
The wish:
"Hindi ko gusto ang tiara pero papayag ako basta si Mama ang maglalagay. Siya lang. Wala nang iba."
(I don't like the tiara but I'll agree to it as long as Mama is the one who puts it on. Only her. No one else.)
The truth:
"Parang hindi ako karapat-dapat sa maraming bagay. Hindi ko alam kung bakit. Palagi na lang ganun — parang may nagtatanggal ng lahat ng tiwala ko sa sarili ko tuwing nagigising ako. Parang hindi ako dapat umupo sa harap. Hindi dapat yung spotlight. Hindi deserving. Gusto ko na si Mama ang maglagay ng korona kasi kung siya ang gagawa — kung siya ang sasabing nandyan ka at importante ka — baka maniwala na rin ako. Kahit sandali."
(I feel like I'm not worthy of many things. I don't know why. It's always like that — like something removes all my self-worth every time I wake up. Like I shouldn't be in front. I shouldn't have the spotlight. Not deserving. I want Mama to put on the crown because if she does it — if she's the one who says you're here and you matter — maybe I'll believe it too. Even just for a moment.)

Entry 6 — The Sash
The wish:
"'Anak' lang ang isulat sa sash. Hindi 'Debutante.' Kahit 18 na ako, gusto ko pa ring maging anak. Gusto ko pa ring pinahihintulutang maging maliit."
(Just write 'Anak' on the sash. Not 'Debutante.' Even at 18, I still want to be a daughter. I still want to be allowed to be small.)
The truth:
"Pagod na pagod na ako. Hindi yung pagod na kailangan lang ng tulog. Yung pagod na malalim — yung parang kahit gising ka, parang natutulog ka pa rin. Yung gusto mong huminto ng lahat — hindi mawala, basta huminto lang sandali. Gusto ko may magdampi sa balikat ko at sabihin na okay lang na hindi okay. Na hindi mo kailangang maging lakas palagi. Na puwede kang maging anak — na puwede kang pangalagaan — kahit gaano na kalaki."
(I am so, so tired. Not the kind of tired that sleep fixes. The deep kind — the kind where even when you're awake, you feel like you're still asleep. The kind where you want everything to stop — not disappear, just stop for a moment. I want someone to put their hand on my shoulder and tell me it's okay not to be okay. That you don't always have to be strong. That you can be someone's child — that you can be taken care of — no matter how old you are.)

Entry 7 — The Last Entry
Written differently from the others. No debut wish at the top. Just the truth, from the first line.
"Isinulat ko lahat ng ito dahil wala akong ibang lugar para ilagay ang totoo. Hindi ko alam kung may makakabasa nito — baka wala. Baka ako lang. Pero kung may makakabasa — kahit sino — gusto ko lang malaman nila na may gusto pa ako. May listahan pa ako ng pangarap. Nagpaplano pa ako ng debut, ng kanta, ng rosas, ng korona. Kaya huwag isipin na wala na akong pag-asa. Mayroon. Nandoon lang siya sa likod ng lahat ng pagod. Nag-aantay."
"Gusto ko lang maabot ng ika-18. Gusto ko lang makita si Mama na ngumingiti. Gusto ko lang marinig yung kanta namin nang sabay — in person, sa isang maliwanag na kwarto, nang walang nararamdamang bigat sa dibdib."
"Kung hindi ko maabot yun — kung mapagod na ako bago pa — sana mabasa ito ni Mama. Sana malaman niya na hindi siya ang dahilan. Sana malaman niya na minahal ko siya sa paraang hindi ko alam kung paano sabihin nang harapan. Sana — kahit wala na ako — ipagdiriwang pa rin niya ang araw. Dahil deserving pa rin ako. Kahit patay na ako. Deserving pa rin."
(I wrote all of this because I had nowhere else to put the truth. I don't know if anyone will read this — maybe no one. Maybe just me. But if someone does — anyone — I want them to know that I still wanted things. I still had a list of dreams. I was still planning a debut, a song, roses, a crown. So don't think I had no hope. I did. It was just hiding behind all the exhaustion. Waiting.)
(I just wanted to reach eighteen. I just wanted to see Mama smile. I just wanted to hear our song together — in person, in a bright room, without this weight in my chest.)
(If I don't get there — if I get too tired before then — I hope Mama reads this. I hope she knows she wasn't the reason. I hope she knows I loved her in ways I never knew how to say out loud. I hope — even if I'm gone — she still celebrates the day. Because I'm still deserving. Even if I'm already dead. Still deserving.)

The mother finishes reading.
She closes the diary.
She holds it against her chest.
Then she places it at the grave — next to the photograph, next to the roses, next to the eighteen candles still burning — and she sits down in the grass in her good dress, and she stays.

What This Changes
The reveal narration now carries a fourth line:
Hindi siya umabot ng ika-18 na kaarawan niya.
She did not make it to her eighteenth birthday.
Tatlong buwan bago ang kanyang debut, namatay siya.
Three months before her debut, she died.
Ngunit dumating pa rin ang araw.
But the day still came.
At binasa ng kanyang nanay ang bawat salita — kasama na ang mga salitang takot siyang sabihin nang buhay pa siya.
And her mother read every word — including the ones she was too afraid to say while she was still alive.

The Philippine Hidden Message
The daughter didn't tell anyone. Not because no one would have listened — but because she had learned, the way young Filipinos learn, that there are feelings you carry quietly. That "huwag mag-drama" is a reflex. That "mag-pray ka lang" is a prescription. That making your mother worry is its own kind of failure.
The diary was her only room with no one else in it.
The game doesn't name what she had. It doesn't need to. The entries name it clearly enough — the colorlessness, the exhaustion that sleep doesn't fix, the feeling of watching life from outside a window, the wanting to stop without wanting to disappear. Filipino players who have felt this will recognize it immediately. Not as a clinical description. As their own handwriting.
The hidden message: we don't see the people we love as they truly are — not because we don't love them enough, but because they learned to hide it, and we never taught them they didn't have to.
The mother's celebration is the answer the daughter never got while she was alive: I see you. All of you. And you were still deserving. You still are.
